Learning about the American voter

The voting public in 2016 is firmly divided. The number of people who are truely independent, those who's vote is honestly up for grab, shrinks every election cycle. This makes it increasingly difficult for people and politicians to engage in honest discussions over policy. Instead they find more success pandering to established, fine tuned partisan messages. Public debate has been replaced with shoring up support.

It's with this in mind that we set out to find a new way to look at voters. With this project we hope to disrupt the two party model of electoral politics in favor of an individualized and issues-based future.

How we made this work

In the book Predisposed, UNL Professor John Hibbing argues that the things that separate liberals and conservatives are not where they live or how they're raised but rather something more fundamental. Indeed this is an emerging cross-sectional field of study between Psychology, Genetics, and Political Science. In a 2011 review Jost et al. wrote "Many studies involving quite diverse samples and methods suggest that political and religious views reﬂect a reasonably strong genetic basis..."

We decided to take it a step further. We wanted to know if you could learn anything about a person's politics by just looking at their face. We surveyed 39 people, mostly in New York City, using the two-dimensional questionnaire from The Political Compass. After receiving their results we ran photos of their faces through Google's Vision API. We ran regressions comparing distances between facial features and their social and economic scores.

Our base data

Using the SciPy libraries, including Pandas and NumPy, we wrote loops that found the euclidean distances between each respondent's 34 identified facial features. That data was run through NumPy's regression functions and then sorted with Pandas to find the most highly correlated relationships. What we found was that the distance between the center of the right eye and its left corner combined with the measurements for the left eye and its pupil were most highly correlated with economic scores. For social scores the bottom of the lower lip and the center of the mouth replaced the right eye distance.

Facial Landmarks

  • Google's Vision API returns 34 facial landmarks. We found the distance between each landmark and tried to correlate them with the scores of the person.
